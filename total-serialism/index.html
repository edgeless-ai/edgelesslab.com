§ 01 · The Constraint

Every algorithm in this catalog obeys a single rule: the output must be drawable by a pen plotter. One continuous or segmented path. No fills. No gradients. No raster tricks. Just a nib dragging ink across paper, tracing whatever geometry the code produced. That sounds limiting. It is limiting. And the limitation is the entire point.

Flow fields become spidery, their smooth continua reduced to discrete strokes that shiver across the page like iron filings near a magnet. Fractals lose their infinite resolution and become meditative instead, each recursive branch a physical mark the plotter chose to make. Reaction-diffusion patterns, which look robust on screen, become fragile on paper. The pen barely touches the surface. The ink pools at turning points. The result feels alive in a way the pixel version never did.

What I did not expect was how the constraint would change my relationship with the algorithms themselves. When you know the output will be physical, you start thinking about the pen's journey. You optimize for travel distance, for stroke order, for the moment the nib lifts and sets down again. The math is the same. The attention is different. You are no longer rendering an image. You are choreographing a drawing.

The constraint also kills certain categories of laziness. On screen, you can hide mediocre composition behind color. You can mask poor line quality with anti-aliasing. You can add drop shadows. On paper, with a single-color pen, there is no place to hide. The algorithm either produces a compelling arrangement of lines or it does not. This is why the catalog exists: to collect the ones that survived the constraint, the generators whose output is worth the ink.

§ 02 · The Categories

Twelve families. Each one a different way of thinking about lines on paper. The taxonomy is imperfect. Some algorithms straddle two categories. A Perlin-noise flow field is also, in a sense, an image-processing algorithm if you feed it a photograph as its vector source. A fractal tree is organic growth. The categories are not walls; they are neighborhoods.

What they share is a bias toward plottability. Every SVG specimen below was generated by one of the 96 algorithms in the toolkit, exported as a single-layer SVG with no fills or gradients, and verified against a physical AxiDraw A3 test run. If it appears here, it has been drawn on paper at least once.

§ 03 · Three That Surprised Us

Most algorithms do exactly what you expect. You tune the parameters, preview the output, send it to the plotter, and the result matches the screen. These three did not. They produced something that only existed because the output was physical, because ink and paper have properties that pixels do not.

Anomaly 01

The algorithm that drew silence

I ran a reaction-diffusion variant with the feed rate turned almost to zero. On screen it looked like a sparse field of pale blobs. I sent it to the plotter anyway, expecting nothing remarkable.

The pen barely touched the paper. Each dot was a single, barely-there contact. Where the simulation had diffused ink into near-transparency, the plotter translated that into near-absence: the nib hovering, descending for a fraction of a second, lifting again. The finished piece had more white space than mark. It looked like a page of Braille written for a language nobody speaks.

Sometimes the most interesting thing an algorithm can draw is almost nothing.

I have run louder algorithms since, but none that taught me more. I keep the piece pinned above the plotter as a reminder. Subtraction is a valid output.

Anomaly 02

The algorithm that drew time

There is a strange attractor in the catalog, number 47, based on a modified Lorenz system. Its orbital paths look similar in every rendering. But on the plotter, something unexpected happened: the pen speed became part of the drawing.

Where the attractor moves slowly through phase space, the pen lingered, depositing more ink, creating darker, thicker strokes. Where the attractor accelerates between lobes, the pen raced across the paper, leaving thin, ghostly trails. The dynamics of the system were encoded not in the geometry but in the ink density.

The drawing contains its own velocity field, if you know how to read it.

I did not program this. The plotter's constant feed rate, combined with varying path segment lengths, produced it automatically. It was a kind of accidental data visualization: the system dynamics encoded in ink weight rather than geometry. Now I look for this effect in every attractor I plot.

Anomaly 03

The one the plotter refused

Algorithm 72 generates dense Islamic-inspired tessellations. The version I liked best produced roughly 50,000 line segments. I queued it, walked away, and came back four hours later to find the plotter had stopped at about 60 percent completion.

The pen nib had worn down to nothing. The last thousand strokes were invisible, scratching grooves into the paper with a dry metal tip. I replaced the nib and considered restarting. Then I looked at the piece.

The transition from ink to inklessness was gradual and beautiful. The tessellation faded from solid black geometry into a ghost grid of embossed lines you could only see in raking light. It looked intentional. It looked better than the complete version would have.

Some of the best algorithmic art is the work the machine declined to finish.

I framed it as-is. I have since re-run the algorithm with a fresh nib and let it complete. The finished version hangs next to the failed one. Visitors always prefer the failure.

Every generator in the toolkit accepts a random seed. Fix the seed and you get the exact same output, every time, on any machine. Change it and you get a variation. This is the fundamental bargain of procedural art: infinite variety within a defined envelope. Ninety-six envelopes. Uncountable variations.

The SVG export is single-layer, single-color, and optimized for plotter travel distance. Lines are sorted to minimize pen lifts. Duplicate segments are removed. The resulting file is ready for saxi, vpype, or any G-code sender that accepts SVG input. No manual cleanup required.
